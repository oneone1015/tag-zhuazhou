What is Zhuazhou? (抓周)

In Taiwanese and Chinese tradition, a baby's first birthday is celebrated with a special ritual called Zhuazhou (pronounced "jwah-joe"). Objects are spread out in front of the baby, and whatever she reaches for first is said to reveal her destiny — her talents, career, or character.

How does it work?

Items representing different life paths are laid out on a mat or table. The baby crawls or toddles toward the display, completely unguided, and grabs whatever catches her eye. No steering allowed — it's all up to her!

Some families believe it's prophetic. Others just love watching a baby make her grand declaration. Either way, it's delightful.

🚪 The Clever Gate (過聰明門)

Before the Zhuazhou grab, many Taiwanese families perform a ritual called Guò Cōngmíng Mén — literally "passing through the clever gate." The baby is carried through an arch or doorway decorated with symbolic items, believed to bless her with intelligence, good health, and a bright future.

Two items are especially important in this ritual:

🧅
Green Onion (蔥, cōng)

In Mandarin, 蔥 (cōng) sounds like 聰 (cōng), meaning clever. Passing the baby over green onions is a wish for her to grow up sharp, quick-witted, and intellectually gifted.

🥜
Peanut (花生, huāshēng)

花生 literally means "blooming life." Peanuts symbolize longevity, abundance, and the wish that the baby will live a long, flourishing life — because the plant blooms above ground but bears fruit below, hidden and enduring.

🪄
The Arch (門, mén)

The "gate" itself represents a threshold — a passage from infancy into a life full of possibility. Being carried through it is a symbolic blessing, a family's collective wish whispered into the new year of her life.
